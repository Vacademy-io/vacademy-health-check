# The Legal and Procedural Framework of Forest Offence Management in Telangana: From Preliminary Detection to Judicial Prosecution

The protection and stewardship of forest resources in the State of Telangana are underpinned by a sophisticated legal and administrative architecture designed to balance environmental conservation with the rigorous enforcement of statutory laws. Central to this framework is the Telangana Forest Act, 1967, a consolidated piece of legislation that seeks to preserve the state's biological wealth while providing a clear mechanism for addressing forest-related crimes.<sup>1</sup> The management of these resources is not merely an administrative task but a complex legal operation involving a hierarchical structure of forest officers, ranging from frontline patrolling units to high-level policy-making authorities. The procedural lifecycle of a forest offence-beginning with the detection of an illicit act and the filing of a Preliminary Offence Report (POR) and potentially culminating in a judicial charge sheet-is governed by precise statutory rules, most notably the Telangana Forest Offence (Compounding and Prosecution) Rules, 1969.<sup>3</sup> This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the officer hierarchy, the investigative protocols involving panchnamas and seizures, and the transition from administrative settlement to criminal prosecution.

## The Administrative and Enforcement Hierarchy

The Telangana Forest Department operates through a decentralized yet rigid hierarchy where authority and responsibility are distributed according to the geographical and functional needs of forest management. This structure ensures that while the broad policy is set at the state level, the day-to-day enforcement remains localized and responsive to field realities.<sup>4</sup>

### Apex Leadership and Policy Formulation: PCCF and CCF

At the zenith of the departmental structure is the Principal Chief Conservator of Forests (PCCF), who serves as the Head of Forest Forces (HoFF). The PCCF is the primary advisor to the state government on all matters related to forest policy, wildlife management, and the ecological security of the state.<sup>5</sup> The role is not merely administrative; the PCCF oversees the entire enforcement mechanism, ensuring that the department's actions align with national and state conservation goals. Below the PCCF, Additional Principal Chief Conservators of Forests (APCCF) and Chief Conservators of Forests (CCF) manage large territorial or functional circles.<sup>5</sup>

The CCF acts as a regional head, supervising multiple forest circles and ensuring that policy directives are translated into actionable plans at the zonal level. In the context of offence management, the CCF often plays a role in high-level disciplinary matters and the administrative sanction of complex prosecution cases.<sup>5</sup>

### Regional Supervision and Circle Management: The Conservator of Forests

The Conservator of Forests (CF) is the immediate supervisor of the forest divisions within a designated circle. The CF holds significant administrative and disciplinary powers, acting as the bridge between the field divisions and the state headquarters.<sup>7</sup> The CF is responsible for inspecting division offices, verifying the quality of field works, and ensuring that forest laws are being enforced uniformly across the circle.

In legal proceedings, the CF often serves as a revisional or appellate authority. For instance, under Section 44(2-D) of the Act, a Conservator of Forests is empowered to call for and examine the records of any confiscation order passed by an authorized officer (the DFO) to satisfy themselves as to its legality and propriety.<sup>8</sup> This creates a layer of internal check-and-balance, ensuring that the substantial powers of seizure and confiscation are not exercised arbitrarily.

### The Pivot of Forest Administration: Divisional Forest Officer (DFO)

The Divisional Forest Officer (DFO), also referred to as the Deputy Conservator of Forests (DCF), is arguably the most critical rank in the enforcement of the Telangana Forest Act. The DFO is the executive head of a forest division, responsible for the protection, conservation, and development of all forest land within that jurisdiction.<sup>5</sup>

In the context of offence management, the DFO functions as the "Authorized Officer" for confiscation proceedings. When timber or other forest produce is seized, it is the DFO who must be satisfied that a forest offence has been committed before ordering the forfeiture of the property to the state.<sup>8</sup> The DFO also manages the division's budget, oversees the staff's conduct, and is the primary authority for compounding serious forest offences that exceed the monetary limits of subordinate officers.<sup>11</sup>

### Field Execution and Range Management: Range Forest Officer (RFO)

The Range Forest Officer (RFO) is the officer in executive charge of a forest range. The RFO is the primary investigator and the face of the department's enforcement in the field. They are responsible for the execution of all sanctioned works, the custody of government property, and the discipline of the subordinate field staff.<sup>13</sup>

The RFO's role in the legal process is multifaceted. They are empowered to compound offences where the value of the produce is relatively low (up to Rs. 50,000 in certain cases), and they are often the authority that decides whether a case should be settled through compensation or pursued in a criminal court through the filing of a charge sheet.<sup>11</sup> The RFO also coordinates the deployment of patrolling units and ensures that the Preliminary Offence Reports generated by field staff are followed by diligent investigations.

### The Frontline: Forest Section Officer and Beat Officer

The most localized level of the department consists of the Forest Section Officer (FSO) and the Forest Beat Officer (FBO), formerly known as the Forest Guard. The FSO (often holding the rank of Forester) supervises a group of beats and is responsible for patrolling, maintaining boundary marks, and preventing encroachments within their section.<sup>13</sup> An FSO is typically the lowest rank empowered to conduct a formal enquiry into a forest offence.<sup>11</sup>

The Forest Beat Officer (FBO) is the frontline protector of the forest. Their primary duty is constant patrolling to detect illegal activities such as poaching, timber smuggling, and unauthorized clearing of land. The FBO is the officer who usually first detects an offence and initiates the formal legal process by recording the incident in the prescribed Form-A, which constitutes the POR.<sup>12</sup>

## The Preliminary Offence Report (POR) Process

The registration of a Preliminary Offence Report (POR) is the formal commencement of the legal journey in a forest offence case. It serves a function similar to the First Information Report (FIR) in general criminal law, although with distinct statutory characteristics.<sup>16</sup>

### The Genesis of Form-A

When a forest officer, typically the Beat Officer or Section Officer, detects a forest offence, they must immediately document the incident. This initial documentation is done using **Form-A**, as prescribed under the Telangana Forest Offence (Compounding and Prosecution) Rules, 1969.<sup>12</sup> The Form-A is the POR. It must contain the specific details of the alleged crime, including the locality, date, and time of detection, the identity of the accused (if known), and a meticulous list of the property seized.<sup>12</sup>

The POR serves two primary purposes: it acts as a permanent record of the state's intent to pursue a legal remedy, and it provides the basis for the subsequent administrative or judicial actions. It is crucial to understand that forest officers are not classified as "police officers" under the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC). Therefore, the POR does not automatically trigger the same investigative mandates as an IPC FIR but instead operates within the specialized legal silo of the Forest and Wildlife Acts.<sup>16</sup>

### Procedural Nuances and Legal Status

The legal weight of a POR has been the subject of significant judicial scrutiny in Telangana. The High Court has clarified that while forest officials are empowered to register a POR for offences under the Telangana Forest Act or the Wildlife (Protection) Act, they lack the jurisdiction to investigate cognizable offences under the Indian Penal Code (IPC), such as criminal intimidation or assault on a public servant.<sup>18</sup> In cases where a forest offence is accompanied by IPC violations, the forest department must proceed with the POR for the forest-specific charges while involving the local police for the IPC-related investigation.<sup>16</sup>

The POR process involves a rapid sequence of events: detection, seizure, marking of property, and immediate reporting. Under Section 44(2) of the Act, every officer seizing property must place a mark on it indicating the seizure and, unless the offender agrees to compound the offence immediately, must report the seizure to the Magistrate or the Authorized Officer without unreasonable delay.<sup>8</sup>

## Investigation, Seizure, and Evidence Gathering

Once a POR is booked, the investigation phase begins. This phase is critical for establishing the "reason to believe" that an offence was committed, which is the legal standard required for both criminal prosecution and administrative confiscation.<sup>21</sup>

### The Panchnama and Search Protocols

The Panchnama is the most vital evidentiary document during the search and seizure process. It is a record of the facts perceived by independent witnesses (Panchas) in the presence of the investigating officer.<sup>22</sup> Although not explicitly defined in the Forest Act, the Panchnama follows the procedural standards set out in Section 100 of the CrPC.

A professionally drawn Panchnama in a forest case must include:

- **Selection of Panchas**: At least two independent and respectable inhabitants of the locality must witness the search. They must not be employees of the department or have a vested interest in the case.<sup>22</sup>
- **Detailed Narration**: The document must describe the scene of the offence, the exact location where the timber or produce was found, and the condition of the site (e.g., fresh stumps, tire marks).<sup>23</sup>
- **Property Description**: Seized articles must be described by their measurements, quantities, and unique identification marks.<sup>12</sup>
- **Signatures**: The Panchas, the accused (if present), and the investigating officer must sign every page of the Panchnama to ensure its authenticity.<sup>22</sup>

### Seizure Reporting: Form-B and Form-C

The physical seizure of property-whether it is forest produce, tools, vehicles, or cattle-triggers the requirement for formal reporting. Following the seizure and the marking of the property, the investigating officer must submit a report of the seizure to the DFO and the Magistrate using **Form-B**.<sup>12</sup> This report must be submitted "immediately" or "without unreasonable delay" to prevent any legal challenges regarding the propriety of the seizure.<sup>8</sup>

Once property is seized, it must be kept in safe custody until the final disposal of the case. **Form-C** is used as a certificate of safe custody, typically signed by the Forest Guard or Watcher who takes responsibility for the items.<sup>11</sup> This ensures a clear chain of custody, which is vital if the case proceeds to a judicial trial.

### The Role of Visual Evidence and Technology

In recent years, the Telangana Forest Department has revolutionized its evidence-gathering process through the integration of digital technology. Field officers are now equipped with smartphones that allow for high-resolution photography and videography of the crime scene and the seized produce.<sup>27</sup> These photographs are often geotagged, providing irrefutable evidence of the location and time of the seizure.

The "Srikonda Model" in Nizamabad is a prime example of this data-driven approach. By using GIS (Geographic Information System) applications and satellite imagery, the department can track patrols, analyze forest fire patterns, and monitor illegal encroachments in real-time.<sup>28</sup> This technological layer complements the traditional Panchnama, making the case for the prosecution significantly more robust by providing a clear, timestamped visual record that minimizes the possibility of witness manipulation or claims of evidence tampering.<sup>29</sup>

## Administrative Adjudication: Compounding of Offences

The Telangana Forest Act provides a unique mechanism for resolving offences without necessarily proceeding to a criminal trial. This is known as "compounding" or "composition" of offences, a power granted under Section 59 of the Act.<sup>2</sup>

### The Mechanism of Composition

Compounding is an administrative settlement where the accused person agrees to pay a sum of money as compensation for the offence committed, in exchange for which the department drops the criminal charges.<sup>32</sup> This process is highly structured to prevent abuse and ensure that it is used only for appropriate cases.

- **Willingness to Compound**: The accused must voluntarily express their willingness to settle the case. This is documented through a written undertaking in **Form-D**, also known as the Compounding Statement.<sup>11</sup>
- **The Order of Composition**: If the competent officer agrees to the settlement, they pass a formal order in **Form-E**. This order specifies the amount of compensation (C-Fee) to be paid and the fate of the seized property (whether it is to be released or forfeited).<sup>12</sup>
- **Monetary and Statutory Limits**: The power to compound is limited by rank. A Range Forest Officer can compound offences where the value of the produce does not exceed Rs. 50,000.<sup>12</sup> However, certain serious offences, such as those involving Sandalwood, Red Sanders, or the use of fast-moving motorized vehicles, are expressly excluded from the RFO's compounding powers and must be handled by the DFO.<sup>11</sup>

### Legal Implications of Compounding

Compounding has significant legal consequences. Under Section 68 of the Act (parallel to Section 59), once an offence is compounded, the accused and the property are discharged, and "no further proceedings" can be taken against them.<sup>32</sup> This finality is crucial for reducing the burden on the judicial system. However, it is important to note that while compounding avoids a criminal conviction, it does not technically mean the person is innocent; rather, it is an administrative admission of liability and a settlement to avoid litigation.<sup>31</sup>

## Judicial Prosecution and the Chargesheet

If an offender refuses to compound the offence, or if the offence is of a nature that the department considers too severe for administrative settlement, the case must be prosecuted in a court of law. This transition from an administrative investigation to a judicial trial is marked by the filing of a charge sheet.<sup>12</sup>

### Transition to the Magistrate Court

The filing of a charge sheet in a forest case follows the completion of the formal enquiry by an officer not below the rank of Forester. The charge sheet is submitted to the Magistrate having jurisdiction over the area using **Form-F**.<sup>12</sup>

The Form-F is a comprehensive document that details the entire history of the case. It must include:

- **The Factual Narrative**: A detailed account of when, where, and by whom the offence was detected.<sup>12</sup>
- **The Legal Framework**: Specific sections of the Telangana Forest Act and the relevant Rules that have been contravened.<sup>12</sup>
- **Witness List**: The names and addresses of all witnesses, along with the specific points on which their evidence is being offered.<sup>12</sup>
- **Enquiry History**: A record of the statements made by the accused and the witnesses during the initial enquiry phase.<sup>12</sup>

### Trial Procedures and Presumptions

Forest trials are often subject to summary procedures under Section 57 of the Act, allowing for a more efficient judicial process.<sup>2</sup> A critical element of these trials is the statutory presumption under Section 60, which states that until the contrary is proved, all timber or forest produce involved in a forest offence is presumed to be the property of the state or central government.<sup>2</sup> This presumption shifts the burden of proof to the accused to establish their legal right to the produce, which is a powerful tool for the prosecution in cases of illegal felling or transit.

## Confiscation of Property and Appeals

Independent of the criminal trial for the offender, the Act provides for the confiscation of the property involved in the offence. This is an _in rem_ proceeding (against the property) designed to ensure that illegal timber and the tools used to obtain it do not return to the illicit market.<sup>8</sup>

### The Confiscation Proceeding (Section 44)

The Authorized Officer (typically the DFO) initiates confiscation proceedings. This is an administrative process that must adhere to the principles of natural justice. Under Section 44(2-B), no order of confiscation can be passed unless:

- **Notice**: A written notice is given to the person from whom the property was seized, informing them of the grounds for confiscation.<sup>34</sup>
- **Representation**: The owner is given a reasonable time to submit a written representation.<sup>34</sup>
- **Hearing**: The owner is given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in person or through an authorized representative.<sup>34</sup>

If the property involves a vehicle, the owner has a statutory defence if they can prove to the satisfaction of the authorized officer that the vehicle was used for the offence without their "knowledge or connivance".<sup>34</sup>

### The Appellate Path

The law provides for several layers of appeal and revision to protect against administrative overreach.

- **Suo Motu Revision**: A Conservator of Forests can call for and review any order of confiscation within 30 days of the order.<sup>8</sup>
- **Judicial Appeal**: Under Section 44(2-E), any person aggrieved by a confiscation order can appeal to the District Court within 30 days of the order being communicated to them.<sup>34</sup> This judicial oversight ensures that the administrative power of the forest department remains subject to the scrutiny of the civil courts.

## Jurisdictional Clarity: Forest Officers vs. Police Officers

One of the most significant areas of legal evolution in Telangana is the clarification of the jurisdictional boundaries between forest officials and the state police. Recent judgments from the Telangana High Court have established that forest officers are not police officers within the meaning of the CrPC.<sup>18</sup>

### The Impact of the Sai Rohit Ruling

In the case of _Kolichelimi Sai Rohit v. State of Telangana_, the High Court dealt with a POR that included sections of the IPC for an alleged assault on forest watchers. Justice J. Sreenivas Rao ruled that forest officials are entitled to initiate proceedings and conduct investigations strictly for offences under the Forest and Wildlife Acts.<sup>16</sup> However, they lack the "inherent jurisdiction" to investigate general penal offences under the IPC.

The court held that if forest officials attempt to register an FIR or a charge sheet under the IPC, the process is "fundamentally defective" and an "abuse of the process of law".<sup>18</sup> This ruling protects the rights of the accused by ensuring that only empowered police authorities conduct investigations into serious penal crimes, while also forcing the forest department to maintain professional focus on the technical aspects of conservation law.

## The Role of the Forest Settlement Officer (FSO)

While most forest officers deal with the protection of existing forests, the **Forest Settlement Officer (FSO)** is a specialized role involved in the creation of Reserved Forests. This role must be distinguished from the Forest Section Officer, despite the shared acronym.<sup>37</sup>

The FSO is usually a revenue official appointed to adjudicate the rights of local people when land is being declared a Reserved Forest under Section 4. Their duties involve:

- **Proclamation**: Publishing a notice in the local vernacular at the headquarters of the Taluka and in every town/village adjoining the land.<sup>37</sup>
- **Enquiry**: Inquiring into all claims related to rights of way, pasture, or forest produce.<sup>37</sup>
- **Compensation**: Determining whether to admit a claim, reject it, or provide compensation if the right cannot be maintained without compromising the forest's ecological integrity.<sup>2</sup>

The FSO process ensures that the expansion of forest cover does not occur through the arbitrary displacement of local communities, providing a structured legal path for the settlement of private rights.<sup>4</sup>

## Conclusion and Future Trajectory

The management of forest offences in Telangana is a highly regulated process that seeks to achieve environmental justice through a combination of administrative flexibility (compounding) and judicial rigor (prosecution). The hierarchical structure of the department, from the Beat Officer's initial detection to the DFO's confiscation orders, provides a clear chain of accountability.

As the department continues to integrate advanced technologies-such as the Srikonda Model's GIS-based patrolling and real-time digital reporting-the efficiency of evidence gathering is poised to increase significantly. However, the legal landscape is also becoming more demanding, as evidenced by High Court rulings requiring a strict separation of forest and police powers. For the professional forest officer in Telangana, the future lies in mastering both the technical tools of conservation and the precise procedural requirements of the law, ensuring that every POR and charge sheet is legally watertight and administratively sound. The synthesis of traditional field skills with modern forensic and legal knowledge remains the most effective path toward the long-term protection of the state's natural heritage.
