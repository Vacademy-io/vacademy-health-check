Pre-Segregation AI Model Objectives
The key goal is to deploy an AI model that sorts plastic bales by polymer type, color, and brand to improve recycling quality and yield.

Bala Krishna Mohan Adusumilli explained that the pre-segregation phase targets sorting mixed PET bottle bales into color-specific and polymer-specific bins to reduce contamination and operational overheads for recyclers (04:13)

This sorting raises yield and quality by delivering cleaner, brand-specific material to recyclers.
The AI model must identify polymer type, color, and brand for each bottle passing on the conveyor.
Weight estimation is a secondary feature, aimed at ±5% accuracy based on surface area rather than exact measurement.
The sorted material is then re-baled and sent to recyclers specializing in specific polymers or brands, enhancing supply chain precision.
The AI model will be deployed at two points: the entry conveyor pre-segregation and the exit conveyor post-segregation to ensure sorting accuracy and quality control (16:21)

At the second point, brand identification and weight accuracy become more critical since the mix is already partially sorted.
A tolerance threshold of 5% error is set; exceeding this triggers retraining of the model to maintain sorting standards.
Data Requirements and Model Training
High-quality, labeled image data reflecting real operational conditions is essential for training the AI model to achieve practical accuracy levels.

Shreyash emphasized the need for at least 1,000 labeled images with annotations for brand, color, polymer type, and weight, covering diverse backgrounds and lighting seen in real sorting environments (20:03)

Data must represent practical scenarios to ensure model robustness across client sites, including complex backgrounds and varied lighting.
Images will be split into training and testing sets to validate model accuracy.
The largest recycler in the country, Sri Chakra, can supply ample varied data, exceeding thousands of tons over months, alleviating data scarcity concerns (21:46).
Bala Krishna Mohan Adusumilli confirmed data availability is the easiest part of the project given Sri Chakra’s extensive materials and variety, offering full control to provide training data on demand (27:54)

He proposed the possibility of delivering 20 tons of material to the development site for hands-on training if needed.
Focus will remain on setting up the environment and pipeline for effective data flow into the model training process.
Technical Infrastructure and Model Selection
The initial infrastructure will rely on edge devices near the conveyor for processing, with a gradual move toward in-house AI development.

Shreyash outlined two main deployment options: GPU servers or edge devices like Jetson Nano or Orin, recommending edge devices for initial low-latency processing near the data source (24:18)

Edge devices reduce network dependency and speed up inference by processing data locally.
GPU or CPU-based options may be considered later as the system scales or requires more compute power.
Bala Krishna Mohan Adusumilli agreed edge deployment is the best starting point to contain costs and complexity, with a plan to transition to higher-end GPUs on demand once the model matures (25:43)

Hardware responsibility for cameras and conveyor integration will be handled by Sri Chakra, while software and computing capacity planning will be driven by the AI team.
For model architecture, Shreyash recommended using advanced detection models like YOLO over basic CNNs to handle multiple bottles in one image, reflecting the practical sorting environment (26:06)

Bala clarified that existing shared models were for reference only, and the final technology will be selected based on clear project requirements.
Project Timeline and Accuracy Expectations
The project aims for a staged accuracy improvement from initial deployment to long-term refinement, balancing feasibility and business impact.

Bala projected an initial deployment accuracy of 70-80% achievable within 3-4 months of training, with gradual improvements toward 80-95% over time (29:59)

Moving from 95% to 99% accuracy is expected to be difficult and time-consuming, involving complex model enhancements.
Fluctuations in accuracy are anticipated due to real-world variations, requiring ongoing retraining to maintain consistency.
The team will focus on reaching a stable 80-95% accuracy range as a practical milestone.
Shreyash agreed to deliver a detailed plan including expected accuracy and model approach within 3-4 days, with a follow-up call tentatively scheduled for Thursday evening to review progress (30:33)

This plan will clarify achievable performance, growth potential, and resource needs.
Both agreed to maintain flexible scheduling to accommodate other projects and priorities.
Strategic Vision for In-House Model Development
The long-term goal is to develop a proprietary AI model, leveraging market solutions initially to build expertise and infrastructure.

Bala shared that a commercial model is being procured from Belgium, expected within two months, to be deployed in a pre-segregation center for learning and benchmarking (18:43)

This will provide foundational knowledge of camera types, conveyor setups, and integration challenges.
Parallel infrastructure will be set up to develop an in-house model based on this experience, aiming for ownership and customization.
The approach balances using existing market technology to jumpstart capability while progressing toward full in-house software development and system control (19:57)

This dual approach reduces risk and accelerates project momentum while preserving long-term independence.
Camera mounting, vibration handling, and conveyor stability are key operational concerns being addressed through this phased rollout.
